Subject-Verb Agreement: Part 2


Key concepts


  • Case 10: This is because a plural verb is employed when the sentence's subject consists of two or more nouns or pronouns joined by and

  • Case 11: A singular verb is used if two subjects are related, connected by and imply the same person or thing.

  • Case 12: When there is more than one verb in a sentence, the verbs must agree with the subject throughout the sentence.

  • Case 13: Uncountable nouns accept a singular verb.

  • Case 14: When a phrase appears between the subject and the verb, remember that the verb still agrees with the subject, not the noun or pronoun in the phrase that follows the subject of the sentence.

  • Case 15: In English,certain countable nouns , like goods, earnings, odds, surroundings, proceeds, contents, and valuables only have a plural form and take a plural verb.

  • Case 16: Collective nouns refer to multiple people or things but are deemed singular and require a single verb. Examples include "group," "team," "committee," "family," and "class."

  • Case 17: A singular verb is used when two or more singular nouns or pronouns are connected by not only/but also, "or"/ "nor," either/or, neither/nor.

  • Case 18: When a compound subject includes both a single and a plural noun or pronoun connected by "or" or "nor," the verb should agree with the part of the subject closest to the verb. This is also known as the rule of proximity.

  • Case 19: Singular indefinite pronouns like "each," "each one," "either," "neither," "everyone," "everybody," "anyone," "anybody," "nobody," "somebody," "someone," and "no one" are subjects in a sentence, necessitate a singular verb.

  • Case 20: Plural indefinite pronouns like several, many, both and few need a plural verb.

  • Case 21: Prepositional phrases between the subject and verb usually do not affect agreement.

  • CASE 22: In most questions, the subject does not appear before the verb. Before deciding on the suitable verb form, be sure you've properly recognised the subject.


New Words!

    Use: The posh lifestyle of the rich and distress of the poor are starkly juxtaposed in these photographs.

  2. Speculative: Engaged in, expressing, or based on conjecture rather than knowledge.
    Use: The detective gave the convict a speculative glance.
